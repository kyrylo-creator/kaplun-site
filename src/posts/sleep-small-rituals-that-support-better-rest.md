Sleep is one of the foundations of both physical and mental wellbeing, yet it is often the first thing we sacrifice when life becomes busy or stressful. It is estimated that around one in three people experience significant sleep difficulties at some point, while chronic insomnia affects approximately 10% of adults. Poor sleep is associated with increased stress, anxiety, reduced concentration, lower emotional resilience, and a higher risk of developing both physical and mental health problems.

Rather than discussing the science of sleep in detail, I would like to share a few habits that have become an important part of my own daily routine. They are simple, but when practised consistently, they can make a meaningful difference.

## 1. Create an Evening Atmosphere

One of the biggest changes I made was replacing bright overhead lighting with softer, warmer lights during the evening. Our brains naturally respond to changes in light, and reducing bright artificial lighting before bedtime helps signal that it is time to slow down and prepare for sleep.

A calm environment also creates a psychological transition between the activity of the day and the rest of the night.

## 2. Reduce Screen Time Before Bed

Phones, computers, and televisions are deeply integrated into modern life, but they can also interfere with healthy sleep. Bright screens, constant notifications, and emotionally stimulating content keep the brain alert when it should be winding down.

Whenever possible, try to avoid emotionally intense news, social media debates, or violent films shortly before bedtime. Instead, choose activities that help your nervous system settle, such as reading, listening to calming music, or simply enjoying a quiet conversation.

## 3. Give Your Body Time to Rest

Eating a large meal immediately before going to bed can make it more difficult for the body to relax and may reduce sleep quality for some people. Many people benefit from finishing their main meal several hours before bedtime, allowing digestion to slow down before sleep.

Like many healthy habits, this often requires planning and establishing a regular daily routine.

## 4. Develop a Personal Evening Ritual

Perhaps the most valuable habit in my own life is meditation before sleep and again after waking.

Meditation is not about "switching off" the mind. Rather, it is a practice of noticing thoughts without becoming carried away by them and gently returning attention to the present moment. Over time, this can help reduce mental tension, improve emotional regulation, and create a greater sense of inner calm.

For some people, prayer serves a similar purpose by creating a regular moment of reflection, gratitude, and peace before sleep. The specific practice matters less than the consistency with which it becomes part of your daily life.

## Consistency Matters More Than Perfection

There is no single technique that guarantees perfect sleep. Healthy sleep is usually the result of many small habits repeated over time rather than one dramatic change.

If sleep difficulties persist for several weeks, significantly affect your daily functioning, or are accompanied by anxiety, low mood, or chronic stress, it may be helpful to seek professional support. Sleep problems are often not only about sleep itself—they can also reflect deeper emotional or psychological processes that deserve attention.

By understanding these underlying factors and developing healthier routines, it is often possible to restore both better sleep and greater overall wellbeing.
